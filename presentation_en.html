Root cause

Each specialist optimizes for herself: she accepts orders that best fit her schedule in terms of distance and timing. But she can't see the full picture — an order near her may have been taken by another specialist who had a better option coming soon after.

The result is an uncoordinated game where everyone optimizes locally and no one optimizes globally. Orders in less popular areas or less convenient time slots are left orphaned.

No one sees the full picture.

Our approach

Plan the entire day at once,
the night before.

The data challenge

To optimize allocation, we need to know where each specialist is, when she works, and what she does. None of this information existed directly in the data.

What was missing
▸ Specialists' home addresses
▸ Declared availability windows
▸ Route between appointments (went direct? returned home?)
▸ Type of transportation used
▸ Whether dedicated to platform or freelancer
▸ Actual maximum daily capacity
What we built from the data
▸ Weighted operational centroid (location proxy)
▸ Availability grid inferred from historical data by day/hour
▸ Behavioral coverage radius (p90 of actual distance)
▸ Speed calibrated against public transit benchmarks

Methodology

How we validated the results.

It's easy to build a model that performs well on data it has already seen. We deliberately made the test harder.

Train / test split

Test

The optimizer never saw March and April data when building the profiles. All results in the following section come exclusively from this test period.

Of the 449 active specialists in the full period, 412 have sufficient history before the cutoff and form the test pool. The remaining 37 started working only in March or had fewer than 2 days of activity before Mar 1. We also report results for the full period (95 days, 449 specialists) which serve as a theoretical upper bound. The difference between the two is ~0.8pp in DR — moderate, reflecting the scarcity of per-weekday data in the 59-day training period.

Parameter robustness

We tested 5 different configurations of capacity limits, radius and travel speed. The DR reduction ranged between 79% and 86% across all configurations — the result does not depend on fine-tuning parameters, but on centralized coordination itself.

Configurations tested: p75–p90 for capacity, p50–p75 for minutes, p75–p90 for radius, 10–12 km/h for transit speed. Details in the sensitivity table available on request.

Model assumptions

Parameters and rules.

We minimized arbitrary assumptions. All parameters are calibrated from the operation's historical data.

Travel model
Public transit — 10 km/h (haversine). Conservative door-to-door speed for public transit in São Paulo. References:
  • SPTrans (Integrated Report 2024): systemic commercial speed 15–16 km/h — in-vehicle time, excluding walking and waiting.
  • CET-SP (2014): 12.1 km/h on non-exclusive lanes — with stops, excluding walking to the stop.
We use haversine distance (straight line), ~1.3x shorter than road distance. 10 km/h on haversine ≈ 13 km/h actual distance — consistent with the references above and conservative as it includes walking and waiting.

Sensitivity test: Running the backtest at 12 km/h instead of 10 km/h changes DR by only 0.08pp (2.20% → 2.12%). Results are robust to the exact speed choice — travel is not the dominant constraint.

Vehicle: 20 km/h for heavy-equipment services — services requiring special equipment.

Distance: Haversine (straight line). Both baseline and optimized use haversine — the relative comparison is fair.

Same address: Orders within 50 m = zero travel. Common for multiple services for the same customer or apartment buildings.

Transition buffer: 15 minutes per transition, limited to 3 per day. Covers setup, going up/down floors and contingencies. Deducted from available time — not "free". The limit of 3 prevents artificial stacking.
Availability grid
For each specialist, we built a per-weekday availability grid. An hour is considered "available" if the specialist had activity in that time slot in at least 20% of occurrences of that weekday in the historical data.

Example: If the specialist worked 14 Thursdays and was active between 2pm–3pm in 8 of them (57%), that slot is available on Thursdays. If she was active 1pm–2pm on only 1 Thursday (7%), that slot is blocked.

Minimum evidence: The per-weekday grid requires at least 2 instances of that day in the history. With 2 instances, the 20% threshold effectively becomes 50% (the specialist needs to have worked in that hour on at least 1 of the 2 occurrences).

Exclusion by absence: Specialists who never worked on a given weekday are excluded from allocations on that day. This eliminates speculative assignments with no direct evidence.

Fallback: When there is only 1 occurrence of a weekday, we use the segment average (weekday or weekend) as a proxy. In practice, 88% of allocations use direct weekday evidence, and 56% have hourly availability of 40% or more — indicating high confidence.
Operational centroid ("where she works")
We don't know where specialists live. Instead of asking, we derive a geographic reference point from their own appointments — the operational centroid.

Calculation: Weighted average of three components: 50% of all appointment locations, 30% of the first appointment of each week, and 20% of the first appointment of each day. The extra weight on first-of-day appointments pulls the centroid closer to where she starts — nearer to home than random mid-day appointments.

Usage: The centroid is the anchor for measuring coverage radius and travel. Both baseline and optimized use the same centroid, ensuring a fair comparison.

Why not ask directly? Because the revealed pattern from data is more robust than declarations. A specialist may provide an outdated address or one different from where she actually operates. The centroid reflects actual behavior.
Certifications and services
Service matching is exact: a specialist only receives orders for services she has already performed in her history. There are 53 distinct service types across a wide range.

We do not assume a specialist can perform a new service. If she has never performed a given service, she doesn't receive orders of that type — even if she is available and nearby.
Capacity limits
Maximum orders/day: 75th percentile of individual daily order history, with a floor of 2. "She handles this volume on 3 out of 4 days." The floor prevents limiting light specialists to 1 order/day.

Maximum minutes/day: 50th percentile (median) of daily total minutes × 1.25 buffer. "Her typical day, with 25% headroom."

Geographic radius: 90th percentile of maximum daily distance from operational centroid. A wider radius gives the optimizer more candidates per order — and, counterintuitively, reduces travel because it enables better geographic packing.

Typical utilization: 66% of specialist-days have slack in both dimensions (orders and minutes). Capacity is not the bottleneck — the dominant constraint is geographic (radius + schedule alignment).

These limits are behavioral, not theoretical. We don't ask anyone to do more than they already do.
Baseline definition
The baseline is what actually happened — the actual appointments and unserved demand recorded in the platform's system. It is not a simulation of the current process; it is the real data.

Completed orders: Those with status "Appointment completed" — the specialist accepted, traveled, and performed the service.

Unserved orders (DR): Those with status "No specialist accepted the appointment" — real demand that found no supply.
Recency filter
Specialists with no activity in the past 30 days are excluded from the candidate pool. This prevents allocations to specialists who may have left the platform, gone on leave, or moved to a different area.

449 specialists pass this filter in the full period. In the test with profiles trained on Jan–Feb, 412 are available (37 started working only in March or had insufficient history before the cutoff).

Path forward

Next steps.

01

Availability validation

Specialists report their actual availability via the app. We cross-reference with the availability grids we inferred from data — this validates the model and improves profile accuracy simultaneously.

02

Pilot with targeted suggestions

A controlled group of specialists receives personalized suggestions: "the 3 best orders for you at this time" — based on the optimizer output. The specialist still has access to the full pool, but gains a recommendation layer that reduces search effort and improves coordination. We measure suggestion acceptance rate and impact on the group's DR.

03

Live data integration

Real-time pipeline: operations sends the order pool, Op-Era returns optimized suggestions. Performance dashboard tracking suggestions vs. reality, projected vs. actual DR, and temporal evolution.

04

Incremental intraday optimization

The lead time analysis (see Methodology) shows that 18% of orders arrive after 7am on the day of service. These orders require a real-time optimization layer that re-optimizes as new orders come in, capturing last-minute demand the overnight batch cannot reach.

05

Per-specialist potential diagnosis

Identify clusters of specialists with similar profiles but divergent utilization — and quantify the latent potential in each group. Foundation for targeted incentives, personalized suggestions, and idle capacity activation.
